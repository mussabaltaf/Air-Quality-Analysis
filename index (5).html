Chapter 01 · AQI Distribution

Where does our data fall on the AQI scale?

With an average AQI of 248.4 and a range from 0 to 499.9, the dataset spans all severity categories. The distribution is notably uniform — each level of air quality is well-represented.

AQI Standard Deviation: ±144.8 — indicating high variance across observations.
Chapter 01 · Health Burden

A tale of two conditions

Across all records, respiratory cases outnumber cardiovascular cases by nearly 2:1. The lungs bear the brunt of air pollution — with total respiratory cases reaching 57,960 compared to 28,991 cardiovascular cases.

Hospital admissions totaled 11,628 — an average of ~2 admissions per record.
Chapter 01 · The Surprising Finding

AQI barely predicts hospital admissions

Despite intuition, the correlation between AQI and hospital admissions is remarkably weak: r ≈ 0.01. Health burden remains consistent across AQI categories — even "Good" air days carry similar case loads.

This suggests that AQI alone is insufficient as a standalone health predictor. Multiple pollutants and lag effects matter more.

Chapter 02 · Pollutant Analysis

Which pollutant
hits hardest?

Six pollutants were tracked: AQI, PM10, PM2.5, NO2, SO2, and O3. While all show weak individual correlations with health outcomes, PM2.5 emerges as the most significant predictor of cardiovascular cases — consistent with global epidemiological evidence.

Chapter 03 · Weather Interactions

Does weather
amplify the danger?

When temperature, humidity, and wind speed are combined with pollution data, subtle interaction effects emerge. Hot + High Humidity conditions show slightly elevated hospital admissions, while high wind speeds appear to offer mild protection through pollutant dispersion.

Summary

Three things the data
tells us

01
AQI Alone Is Not Enough

With a near-zero correlation (r ≈ 0.01) between AQI and hospital admissions, single-index measurements fail to capture the full complexity of air quality health risk. Multi-pollutant indices are needed.

02
PM2.5 Is the Primary Threat

Among all pollutants tracked, fine particulate matter (PM2.5) shows the strongest association with cardiovascular cases — aligning with WHO evidence that these ultrafine particles penetrate deepest into the bloodstream.

03
Weather Creates Compound Risk

Hot, humid conditions mildly amplify health burdens while high wind speeds provide some protection via pollutant dispersion. Public health advisories should account for these compound environmental conditions.
