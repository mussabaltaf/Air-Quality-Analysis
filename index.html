Chapter 01 — AQI Distribution

Air quality across the full spectrum

With a mean AQI of 248.4 and values spanning 0–499.9, our dataset covers every severity level from pristine to hazardous. The histogram reveals a strikingly uniform distribution — roughly 200–260 records fall in each 20-unit bin across the full 0–500 range. No single AQI level dominates the data.

This uniform spread is analytically valuable: equal representation across severity tiers enables fair, unbiased comparisons of health outcomes at every level of air quality.
Chapter 01 — The Health Burden

Lungs bear the heaviest toll

Across all 5,811 records, respiratory cases total 57,960 — nearly twice the 28,991 cardiovascular cases. Hospital admissions totalled 11,628, averaging exactly 2.00 per observation. The scale of respiratory burden is substantial and consistent.

Per-record averages: 9.97 respiratory · 4.99 cardiovascular · 2.00 hospital admissions
Chapter 01 — The Central Finding

The finding that defies intuition

Here is the core puzzle of this dataset: despite AQI spanning from near-zero to hazardous, average hospital admissions barely change across all six categories. From "Good" air to "Hazardous," the difference in avg admissions is less than 0.11. The Pearson correlation is r = −0.014 — effectively zero.

Implication: AQI as a single index is insufficient to predict health burden. Multi-pollutant models, exposure duration, and demographic factors are needed for meaningful predictions.
Chapter 01 — Scatter Analysis

500 data points, no discernible slope

A scatter plot of 500 sampled records — AQI on x, hospital admissions on y — shows a vertical cloud with no trending slope. The regression line is nearly flat at r = −0.014. High-AQI records scatter across the same admission range (0–12) as low-AQI records.

Each dot represents one patient record. Points are coloured by AQI category. The flat trend line is the visual proof of the paradox uncovered in this analysis.

Chapter 02 — Pollutant Analysis

Which pollutant
matters most?

Six pollutants were tracked: AQI, PM10, PM2.5, NO2, SO2, and O3. Pearson correlations against all three health outcomes reveal that PM2.5 (Fine Particulate Matter) has the strongest association with respiratory cases (r = +0.025) — consistent with decades of global epidemiological research showing fine particles penetrate deepest into lung tissue.

Summary of Key Insights

Three things the
data reveals

01
The AQI Paradox

With a near-zero Pearson correlation of r = −0.014 between AQI and hospital admissions, the index alone fails as a health predictor. Avg admissions range from 1.96 to 2.07 across all six severity categories — a difference of merely 0.11, despite AQI spanning 0–500.

02
PM2.5 Is the Primary Signal

Among all tracked pollutants, fine particulate matter (PM2.5) shows the strongest association with respiratory cases (r = +0.025). This aligns with WHO and EPA evidence that PM2.5 particles — smaller than 2.5 microns — bypass respiratory defenses and enter the bloodstream directly.

03
Weather Creates Compound Risk

Hot, low-wind conditions produce the highest Health Impact Scores (95.0), while high-wind scenarios reach a minimum of 92.2. Weather-aware public health advisories — particularly during heat events with poor atmospheric dispersion — could provide meaningful additional protection.
